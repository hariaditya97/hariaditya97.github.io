01 — Open Source

miniROV

Active Development

A tethered underwater ROV with LLM-assisted supervised autonomy, built for UK coastal and lake deployments. The operator issues natural language commands; a locally-hosted LLM interprets them and dispatches structured MAVLink instructions — with full manual override retained at all times. Phase 0 complete: the full NL → Ollama → pymavlink → ArduSub SITL pipeline is validated.

ArduSub MAVLink / pymavlink Ollama · qwen2.5:14b Raspberry Pi 5 Navigator Flight Controller 4× T200 Thrusters Python SITL Simulation

System Architecture

A two-node topology connected over a 50 m tether. Topside: an Apple M4 Pro running Ollama locally for inference, and a Python minirov-brain agent handling command arbitration and safety envelopes. Subsea: a Raspberry Pi 5 running ArduSub via the Blue Robotics Navigator Hat, driving 4× T200 thrusters. No cloud dependency in the inference loop.

NL → MAVLink Pipeline

01
Natural Language Input
Operator issues plain-English commands — e.g. "descend to 3 m and hold heading north."
02
Ollama Inference (qwen2.5:14b)
Locally-hosted LLM parses intent and returns a structured JSON action payload. Zero external API latency.
03
minirov-brain Arbitration
Python agent validates JSON schema, checks safety envelopes, converts to MAVLink RC override messages via pymavlink.
04
ArduSub Execution
RC override commands reach ArduSub Stable-4.1, which handles low-level stabilisation, depth hold, and thruster mixing. Manual override available at all times.

02 — MSc Thesis

Underwater
Glider

Complete

Designed and built a fully deployable underwater glider platform fitted with a nuclear scintillator for oceanic radiation mapping — motivated by post-Fukushima oceanographic research. A complete end-to-end systems engineering project spanning hydrodynamic design, buoyancy engine fabrication, sensor integration, and field trials.

Buoyancy-Driven Propulsion Nuclear Scintillator Hydrodynamic Design Radiation Mapping Embedded Electronics Mechanical Fabrication

Design Rationale

Oceanographic gliders offer unmatched endurance-to-cost ratios for long-duration surveys compared to propeller-driven AUVs. The design prioritised minimal acoustic signature — critical for maintaining signal-to-noise ratio with the scintillator sensor — alongside low drag and practical deployability from a small vessel without specialist launch equipment.

Buoyancy Engine

Propulsion is achieved entirely through buoyancy modulation: a syringe-based hydraulic system drives a variable-volume external bladder, converting vertical motion into forward glide via fixed wings. Pitch attitude is adjusted by translating an internal battery mass along the longitudinal axis, enabling heading changes without external control surfaces and eliminating associated drag.

Radiation Sensing Payload

The payload integrates a nuclear scintillator for in-situ gamma radiation detection. Measurements are timestamped against GPS fix (surface) and pressure-derived depth, enabling geo-referenced, depth-resolved radiation maps of the water column — directly replicating the methodology developed for post-Fukushima long-range oceanic contamination surveys.

03 — Team Project

Tricopter
UAV

Team of Five

As part of a five-person team, designed and manufactured a custom tricopter UAV integrating a servo-actuated thrust vectoring mechanism for yaw control — paired with an in-house pickup and release mechanism capable of delivering a 150 g vaccine payload over a 15-minute flight.

Thrust Vectoring Yaw Servo Actuation UAV Systems 15 min Endurance 150 g Payload Payload Release Mechanism

Yaw Control Architecture

Conventional tricopters use a servo to tilt the aft rotor, generating yaw torque without a counter-rotating propeller pair. This design uses a custom-designed servo-driven vectoring mount on the aft arm, tuned to minimise cross-coupling with roll and pitch channels. The approach avoids the weight and drag penalty of a fourth rotor while retaining aerodynamic efficiency at the design payload fraction.

Payload Delivery System

An in-house pickup and release mechanism was designed for field reliability under vibration loading. The system uses a latched electromagnet activated via flight controller auxiliary channel, enabling a precise, commanded drop from hover. Maximum payload: 150 g — sized for a standard vaccine vial pack.

Specifications

ConfigurationTricopter — 2 fixed + 1 vectored aft
Yaw MechanismServo-actuated thrust vector
Max Payload150 g (vaccine delivery)
Endurance (loaded)15 minutes
Release MechanismLatched electromagnet (FC AUX output)
Team Size5 engineers

Design Challenges

Balancing endurance against payload fraction required careful motor and propeller selection. The vectoring mechanism introduced significant PID tuning complexity — the aft rotor's changing thrust vector affects pitch authority at extremes of servo travel, requiring gain scheduling and mechanical travel limits to prevent attitude excursions.

04 — CFD + Wind Tunnel

3-Wheeler
Aerodynamics

Complete

CFD-based external aerodynamic analysis of a concept 3-wheeler using StarCCM+, achieving a 17% drag reduction (ΔCd) through iterative surface geometry refinement. Results validated experimentally against a geometrically scaled 3D printed model in a wind tunnel.

StarCCM+ RANS CFD Wind Tunnel Testing k-ω SST Surface Refinement 17% Drag Reduction

CFD Methodology

Steady-state RANS simulations in StarCCM+ using the k-ω SST turbulence closure — appropriate for external automotive aerodynamics where adverse pressure gradient boundary layer separation is the primary drag mechanism. C-H block domain topology with targeted refinement volumes around the nose, A-pillars, underbody, and wake. Mesh independence verified across three refinement levels prior to geometry optimisation.

Optimisation Process

Iterative surface refinement targeted the principal drag sources identified from Cp and skin friction distributions: frontal bluff-body pressure drag, A-pillar separation, and underbody wake. Modifications across iterations included nose profile reshaping, A-pillar radius increases, underbody fairing, and rear diffuser angle optimisation. Each iteration assessed for ΔCd and ΔCl to preserve stability margin.

Experimental Validation

A geometrically scaled model was fabricated via FDM 3D printing and tested in a subsonic wind tunnel with a six-axis force balance. Drag and lift measurements at matched Reynolds number were compared against CFD predictions — agreement within acceptable margins confirmed the validity of the numerical approach and the optimisation conclusions.

05 — FSAE Electric

Carbon Fibre
Sidepods

Complete

Led aerothermal design of the lithium battery pack cooling system for a Formula Student electric vehicle. Modelled as forced convection through custom CFRP duct geometry, optimising duct profile to balance aerodynamic drag penalty against cell heat removal requirements under sustained high-discharge conditions.

Aerothermal Design Forced Convection Modelling CFRP Wet Layup Battery Thermal Management FSAE Electric Duct Geometry Optimisation

Thermal Design Problem

Formula Student electric powertrains subject the battery pack to sustained high-discharge thermal loads during dynamic events. The design constraint was to extract sufficient heat from lithium cells using only ram air ducted through the sidepod — no active cooling, no pumps, no added mass beyond the duct structure itself.

Aerothermal Modelling

The system was analytically modelled as internal forced convection over a parameterised finned battery module. Duct geometry swept across inlet area, diffuser half-angle, and area ratio — characterising Nusselt number, pressure recovery, and skin friction drag. The Pareto front between cooling performance and aerodynamic cost identified the design-point geometry for manufacture.

Composite Fabrication

Sidepod skins manufactured in woven CFRP via wet layup over a machined foam core. The inlet lip geometry required complex compound curvature, demanding careful ply orientation selection to maintain structural integrity at the duct throat where local pressure gradients produce the highest bending loads on the skin laminate.
